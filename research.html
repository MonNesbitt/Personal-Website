Research Projects

Thus Far.

My research focuses on the intersection between speech perception, phonological representations and the influence of social factors on these things using experimental methods. My most recent projects are centered around syllabic representations, and the connections between identity and participation in sound change.

Syllabic Representations

Evidence for syllabic representations have remained illusive. This is especially true for so-called ambisyllabic consonants, e.g. the /m/ in lemon. Traditionally, there have been three major analyses for these consonants: they are onsets, codas, or linked to two syllables. Thus far, I have conducted a perception and production task probing syllabic representations in American English. Both suggest that ambisyllabic consonants are codas.

Phonological Changes in Progress

I am also quite fascinated with sound change. I have been conducting research in conjunction with Dr. Wagner and my colleagues in the Michigan State Sociolinguistics Lab. Thus far, our research suggests that the local dialect spoken in Michigan and surrounding states, the Northern Cities Shift, is declining.

Changing Economies and Sound Change

I am particularly interested in the social motivations for such a decline. My research investigates the links, if any, between economic and dialect decline in the rust belt.
